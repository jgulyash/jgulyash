# The THREAT Matrix and WARDEN Ecosystem

THREAT Matrix provides the taxonomy: a structured, shared vocabulary for how threat actors plan and execute adversarial action across people, facilities, organizations, and critical infrastructure. WARDEN operationalizes that taxonomy: an active threat monitoring framework that tracks specific actors against specific principals, scores severity, and supports analysts and operations personnel in making informed, defensible decisions about when and how to respond.

Each tool stands alone. Together they form a practitioner ecosystem with no open-source equivalent in the physical and human threat intelligence space.

**THREAT Matrix names the threat actor and what they do. WARDEN supports the analyst in determining what to do about it.**

---

## Where They Overlap

Both tools operate across the same threat landscape — the same actor types, the same phases of adversarial behavior, the same target populations.

A Fixated Individual (AP001) targeting an executive shows up in THREAT Matrix as a mapped set of tactics across four phases. That same actor shows up in WARDEN as a tracked threat subject with a scoring record and escalation history. A Malicious Insider (AP004) exfiltrating intellectual property shows up in THREAT Matrix under the Organization matrix. That same insider shows up in WARDEN as an active insider risk case with source documentation and a structured response pathway.

The overlap is intentional. Both tools share a common vocabulary and a common understanding of who threat actors are, how they behave, and how their behavior progresses across a threat lifecycle. That shared foundation is what makes them interoperable.

---

## Where THREAT Matrix Feeds Into WARDEN

THREAT Matrix is the intelligence library. WARDEN draws from it operationally in three specific ways.

**Actor profiles as intake categories.** When a new threat subject enters WARDEN, analysts assign them an actor profile from THREAT Matrix. That single classification immediately surfaces what behavioral patterns to expect, which phases that actor type typically executes, and what indicators to monitor. Analysts are not starting from scratch. They are starting from a structured, shared understanding of who this person is and how actors like them behave.

**Tactic progression as a monitoring map.** THREAT Matrix's four-phase structure gives WARDEN a framework for tracking threat escalation over time. Phase 1 (Target Development) looks different from Phase 2 (Approach). When a threat subject moves from reconnaissance behaviors to approach behaviors, that is a defined, nameable transition grounded in the taxonomy, not in analyst intuition alone. WARDEN's scoring model reflects that progression, and phase transitions inform escalation thresholds.

**Cyber-Physical Nexus tagging as a detection bridge.** THREAT Matrix identifies where digital capabilities enable or accelerate physical threat action. WARDEN inherits that intelligence. When monitoring a threat subject in a CPN-tagged phase, analysts know to pull digital indicators alongside physical ones. The taxonomy tells WARDEN where the two domains converge so practitioners know where to look.

## How WARDEN Takes It Further

THREAT Matrix is a reference standard. It names behaviors, maps actors, and structures the space. It is designed to be universal, timeless, and a living document that changes as the threat environment evolves. WARDEN is operational. It tracks specific actors against specific principals, in real time, and produces intelligence that has to hold up under legal, HR, and executive scrutiny.

WARDEN takes the taxonomy and makes it executable in four ways.

**From classification to case management.** THREAT Matrix tells analysts what a Recruited Insider does. WARDEN tracks whether a specific recruited insider has progressed from Phase 1 to Phase 2, who reported it, what sources that intelligence came from, and whether it crosses the threshold for a legal referral.

**From behavior patterns to scored severity.** THREAT Matrix identifies which tactics an actor profile uses. WARDEN scores how far along that progression a specific threat subject is, weighted by the reliability of available intelligence. Analysts can brief a CISO or a Board with a number, a trend, and a recommendation grounded in documented behavior — not narrative alone.

**From shared vocabulary to structured escalation.** THREAT Matrix gives every team member the same language. WARDEN uses that language to structure escalation pathways: when a threat crosses defined thresholds tied to named, documented behaviors, the appropriate notifications and response protocols are clearly defined. Professional judgment drives the decision. The framework ensures that decision is consistent, documented, and defensible.

**From individual cases to institutional memory.** WARDEN's Source Acquisition intake with source attribution and chain-of-custody tracking means every source, every tip, every contact report is documented and attributable. When a case reaches law enforcement, HR, or legal counsel, the intelligence record holds up.

THREAT Matrix gives practitioners the consistent vocabulary and threat lifecycle to describe what happened. WARDEN gives them the platform to operationalize investigations at scale.

---

## For Practitioners

Both tools are MIT licensed. Each functions independently.

THREAT Matrix works as a standalone reference taxonomy for structuring threat assessments, building detection logic, and establishing a shared analytical language across teams. WARDEN works as a standalone threat monitoring framework for any team responsible for tracking and managing threats to personnel and organizational assets.

Used together, they give practitioners a consistent vocabulary from the intelligence library through to the case file. Everyone from the analyst to the CISO is working from the same framework, at every stage of a threat's lifecycle.
